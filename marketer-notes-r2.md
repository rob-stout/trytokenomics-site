# Marketer Notes — Round 2

Three voice shifts and one call I'm unsure about.

---

## Shift 1: Adversarial framing replaced with observational framing

Round 1 hero ("Your AI tools don't warn you. Now your menu bar does") frames the product as a corrective — the tools are failing, Tokenomics fixes the failure. The design system principles ("Calm, not clever") call for a different posture. The new hero ("See where you stand *before* the API decides for you") makes the same claim without appointing a villain. "The API decides for you" is observational: something true happens, Tokenomics puts you ahead of it. The tension is real; the tone is not adversarial.

## Shift 2: Italic accent as emotional anchor, not as emphasis

In Round 1, italics weren't in the vocabulary — the copy worked through sentence structure and word choice alone. The design system makes the italic a first-class design token: it does what an adjective usually does badly. Every headline revision in Round 2 treats the italic word as the one thing the reader should retain from that line, not as decoration on a strong word. This changes which word gets italicized — it's rarely the noun, usually the qualifier or adverb that sets up the tension ("before," "honest," "always," "one").

## Shift 3: Section titles compressed from descriptive to evocative

Round 1 section titles were descriptive ("Know if you're burning fast — before the API tells you to stop") — they summarize the section body. The design system's card examples ("Quiet polling, loud clarity") show a different model: the title names a single idea at the H2/H3 level, the body expands it. Round 2 titles are 4–8 words, carry one italic accent, and leave room for the body to add specificity rather than restating the title.

---

## One Call I'm Unsure About

The pace dot diagram labels use "Even-pace mark" for the dot callout. It's precise — it names what the dot calculates — but "even-pace" is a compound that requires a beat to parse on first read. The alternative is something shorter like "Pace mark" or "Where you'd be" (which is more colloquial but immediately understood). I kept "Even-pace mark" because accuracy matters more than warmth in a diagram annotation, and "Where you'd be" risks sounding vague in a technical context. But if user testing shows confusion at the diagram, the label is the first thing to swap.
